Understanding Parallel Structure in Sentences

A sentence is considered "out of control" if it contains a list of items that disrupts the sentenceâ€™s grammatical consistency, making it nonsensical or ungrammatical.

Example 1

Original Sentence: Mary likes walking, swimming, and to ride a bicycle.

Problem: The sentence mixes gerunds ("walking," "swimming") with an infinitive ("to ride"), leading to inconsistent verb forms.

Corrected Sentence: Mary likes walking, swimming, and riding a bicycle.

Explanation: To maintain parallelism, all items in the list should use the same form. Here, using gerunds ("walking," "swimming") for consistency is appropriate.

Example 2

Original Sentence: Dragons are big, green, and eat people.

Problem: The sentence mixes descriptions with actions in a way that disrupts the parallel structure. It seems to say:

  • Dragons are big.
  • Dragons are green.
  • Dragons eat people (this disrupts the parallelism as the action "eat" is not part of the descriptive list).

Corrected Sentence: Dragons are big, green, and eat people.

Explanation: To maintain parallelism and clarity, the corrected sentence should clearly separate descriptions from actions.
